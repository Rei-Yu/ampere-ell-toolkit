Tool 01 — Voice

Talk with your student in real time.

Speak in English. Your student reads in their language. They speak back. No typing needed.

🌐100+ languages
0Account needed
FreeAlways
⚡Instant translation
Best used for
Welcoming a new student on their first day · Explaining a task or activity · Giving directions · Quick check-ins on how a student is feeling · Any moment where you need to communicate something right now and typing isn't practical.
How to set it up — follow the pictures
STEP 1
Open the website
Open your internet browser and go to translate.google.com. You will see two panels side by side. Click the language name on the right panel and type your student's home language to select it. The left panel should already say English.
STEP 2
Click the microphone and speak
Click the 🎙️ microphone button on the left (English) side. Your browser may pop up a message asking permission — click Allow. Then speak your message clearly in English. The translation will appear on the right side automatically.
STEP 3
Turn the screen toward your student
The translation appears on the right side in the student's language. Turn the laptop or tablet screen so the student can read it. They do not need to type anything — just read the screen.
STEP 4
Swap so the student can speak back
Press the ⇄ Swap button in the middle. Now the right side is the student's language. Have the student click the 🎙️ microphone on the right and speak in their language. Their words appear in English on the left for you to read.
⚠ If the microphone icon is grey and won't click
This is an IT system setting — not a website error. Go to Apple Menu → System Settings → Privacy & Security → Microphone and make sure your browser is switched on. If the setting is locked, contact your IT department.

Tool 02 — Documents

Translate any document in minutes.

Upload a PDF or Word file and get a translated, editable copy — no typing, no copying and pasting.

10MBMax file size
0Account needed
FreeAlways
.docxDownload format
Best used for
Blank class worksheets and activity sheets · Permission forms · School event flyers · General information packages sent home · Any document that does not already have a student's personal information filled in.
How to use it — follow the pictures
STEP 1
Go to the website and click Documents
Go to translate.google.com. At the top of the page you will see tabs that say Text and Documents. Click Documents.
STEP 2
Upload your file
Click the upload area and find your PDF or Word file on your computer. Select it. Important: the file must be smaller than 10 MB or it will not upload.
STEP 3
Set languages and click Translate
Make sure the left box shows English. Click the right box and type the family's language to select it. Then click the big Translate button.
STEP 4
Download as Word (.docx)
When the translation is ready, click Download. Choose .docx (Word format) — not PDF. The Word format lets you open and edit the file to fix any errors.
STEP 5
Open and read before sharing
Open the downloaded file in Word. Read through the translation before handing it to families. Look for any words that seem odd — Google Translate is good but not perfect. Fix anything that looks wrong, then share.
⚠ Two things to watch for
File too large: If your file is over 10 MB, it will not upload. Open the PDF in Preview → Export → Reduce File Size first, or use smallpdf.com (free, no login).

Literal translation errors: Google Translate is very good but not perfect. During field testing, the word "space" in a board game was translated as "outer space" in French instead of the correct game square. Always read the .docx before handing it to families.

Tool 03 — Parent Emails

Write home to families in their language.

DeepL produces more natural, nuanced translations than standard tools — ideal for sensitive parent communication.

✗Redact first
0Account needed
FreeAlways
30+Languages
Best used for
General parent updates · Meeting requests · Homework reminders · Celebration messages · Any written communication going home to families — as long as you follow the Safe Privacy Step first.
✗
Read this before you start — The Safe Privacy Step
DeepL is run by an outside company. Before you paste anything into it, you must remove the student's real name and replace it with the word [Student]. Do the same for any medical or IEP details. This one step keeps you fully protected under school privacy rules.
How to use it — follow the pictures
STEP 1
Write your email as normal
Open your school email and write your message as you normally would. Do not click Send yet. You need to do the privacy step first.
STEP 2 — Privacy First
Replace the student's name
Go through your email and delete the student's real name every time it appears. Type [Student] in its place. Do the same for any medical details or IEP information. Now the email is safe to translate.
STEP 3
Copy and paste into DeepL
Select all the text in your email and copy it (Ctrl+C on Windows, Cmd+C on Mac). Open a new browser tab, go to deepl.com/translator, and paste the text into the left box. Select the family's language on the right.
STEP 4
Copy the translation
DeepL will translate automatically. Click the Copy button to copy the translated text. Then go back to your school email tab.
STEP 5
Paste, restore the name, and send
Paste the translation into your school email. Find every place where it says [Student] and replace it with the student's real name. Double-check the email looks right, then click Send.
⚠ Why DeepL instead of Google Translate for emails?
DeepL produces more conversational, natural-sounding translations — especially for emotional or sensitive topics. When writing home about a student's progress, a concern, or a family event, DeepL's output feels less robotic and more respectful to families reading it.

🎙️ Tool 01 — Voice Conversations translate.google.com
The Bottleneck Summary
Dead mic icon on MacBook Greyed out or unclickable microphone button +
The Fix — Step by Step This is a system-level block, not a website error. Go to Apple Menu → System Settings → Privacy & Security → Microphone and enable your browser. If the toggle is greyed out or locked, the school MDM policy is blocking it — submit an IT request. You cannot fix this from the browser.
Wrong language auto-detected Google Translate picked the wrong language automatically +
The Fix — Step by Step Do not use Detect language — always set the language manually. Click the language name at the top of each panel and type the name to search for it. If you are unsure, ask the student to type a few words and check what Google Translate detects, then switch from auto-detect to that specific language going forward.
📄 Tool 02 — Document Translation translate.google.com/?op=docs
The Bottleneck Summary
10 MB upload rejected Heavy PDF or PowerPoint file fails to upload +
The Fix — Step by Step Compress the file before uploading. For PDFs: open in Preview → File → Export → Reduce File Size. Or use smallpdf.com (free, no account needed). For PowerPoints: delete embedded videos, then compress images via Insert → Pictures → Compress Images. Re-upload once under 10 MB.
Literal translation error "Space" → "outer space" (espace) not a game square (case) +
The Fix — Step by Step Always download the output as .docx (not PDF). Open in Word or Google Docs and use Ctrl+F / Cmd+F → Find & Replace to locate and correct the mistranslated term. For recurring problem words, add a flagged comment in your source template so you remember to check each time.
Broken layout after download Text boxes shifted, columns misaligned in the .docx +
The Fix — Step by Step This happens when the original file uses complex layouts. After downloading the .docx, open in Word and use View → Print Layout to spot shifted elements. Drag any misplaced text box back into position. For heavily formatted worksheets, it is faster to copy only the translated text and paste it manually into your original file, section by section.

FIPPA privacy risk Student name or sensitive data pasted into DeepL +

🧑‍🏫 Human Challenges — When the tool works but the situation is still hard
The Situation What to do
The family's language isn't in Google Translate Language not available or dialect not recognised +
What to do Google Translate supports 130+ languages but not all dialects or minority languages. First, try alternate names — some languages are listed differently (e.g. "Tagalog" instead of "Filipino", "Simplified Chinese" instead of "Mandarin"). If the language genuinely isn't available, contact your school's settlement worker or ELL coordinator — most districts have access to professional interpretation services. MOSAIC BC (mosaicbc.com) and DIVERSEcity offer community interpretation support in Metro Vancouver and the Fraser Valley. Your district's ELL department can also request professional translation for critical documents.
The student is not literate in their home language Student cannot read the translated text on screen +
What to do This is more common than expected, particularly with students from refugee backgrounds, interrupted schooling, or oral-language-dominant communities. If a student cannot read the translation on screen, switch to the Voice tool only — spoken translation is far more accessible than written. Focus on gestures, realia, and visual cues (see the Accessible Input Checklist in the ELL Learners tab). Connect with the school's settlement worker or ELL specialist to understand the student's full literacy background before choosing a communication strategy.
The parent doesn't have a device to read the translated email Family has limited or no access to technology at home +
What to do Email assumes the family has a device and checks it regularly — many newcomer and low-income families do not. If email isn't reaching a family, ask the student what communication works at home (phone call? paper note? WhatsApp?). For phone calls, use the Voice tool on speaker with the family on the line — speak in English and hold the phone to the speaker so the family hears the translation. For paper notes, print the translated .docx from Tool 02 and send it home physically. Some districts also have School Messenger with multilingual SMS options — check with your office.
The translation is correct but the family still seems confused Language is translated but cultural or contextual meaning is lost +
What to do Translation converts words — it does not convert cultural context. Terms like "IEP", "report card", "PAC meeting", or "school district" may translate correctly but mean nothing to a family unfamiliar with the BC school system. When writing to families about these topics, add a plain-language sentence explaining what the term means before translating it. For example: "An IEP (this is a plan the school makes to help your child with specific learning needs) meeting is scheduled for..." Pair written communication with a follow-up check-in using the Voice tool to confirm understanding.
The student speaks a different dialect than the family Student has adapted to English but family still needs support +
What to do It is common for children to acquire English faster than their parents or guardians. Do not rely on the student to translate sensitive communications — this places an unfair burden on the child and may result in information being filtered or softened. For family communication about behaviour, learning needs, or wellbeing, always use an adult interpreter. Your school's settlement worker, district ELL team, or a community organisation can connect you with appropriate support. The translation tools in this dashboard are for educator use — not for asking the student to translate for their own family.

Privacy Rules for Every Tool

Each tool has its own privacy considerations. Know the rules before you use it.

Why privacy matters with free web tools

Under BC privacy law (FIPPA), a student's name, medical information, and IEP content are protected personal data. Free web tools like Google Translate and DeepL are run by outside companies — not your school. Sharing student-identifiable information with any outside service, even by accident and even for a moment, may count as a privacy breach. The rules below keep you fully protected for each tool.

🎙️ Tool 01 — Voice Conversations · translate.google.com

Privacy risk level: Low — with one rule

Google Translate Voice works in real time and does not store your conversations on your device. However, Google's servers do process what is spoken. The rule: use this tool for general classroom communication and student conversations only. Do not use it to discuss a student's medical needs, IEP details, or disciplinary matters out loud in a shared or public space where others can hear.

✗ Do not use for

Discussing IEP goals or accommodations · Medical or mental health conversations · Behavioural incidents · Any conversation a parent or guardian should be part of first.

✓ Safe to use for

Greeting a new student · Explaining a classroom task · Giving directions · Asking how a student is feeling · General check-ins and daily communication.

📄 Tool 02 — Document Translation · translate.google.com/?op=docs

Privacy risk level: Low — check your document first

Google Translate Documents uploads your file to Google's servers for processing. For most classroom worksheets, activity sheets, and general handouts, this is completely fine. The rule: before uploading any document, quickly scan it for student names or personal details. Generic classroom materials with no student-identifying information are safe to upload.

✗ Do not upload

Any document with a student's name already filled in · Report cards · IEP documents · Behaviour reports · Any file containing personal or medical information about a specific student.

✓ Safe to upload

Blank worksheets · Activity sheets · Permission slips with no student names pre-filled · Class newsletters · Curriculum documents · General school information packages.

✉️ Tool 03 — Parent Emails · deepl.com/translator

Privacy risk level: Moderate — redact before every use

DeepL is a third-party tool not affiliated with your school or Google. Because parent emails often contain student names and sensitive details, you must follow the redaction step every single time before pasting text into DeepL.

The Safe Privacy Step — 4 actions, every time

Step 1 — Write your email draft as normal in your school email program.

Step 2 — Before copying anything, erase all personal details and replace them:
"Marcus Johnson" → type "[Student]" in its place
"ADHD diagnosis" → type "[medical detail]" in its place
"IEP accommodation for reading" → type "[support detail]" in its place

Step 3 — Copy only the version with [Student] into DeepL. Translate. Copy the result.

Step 4 — Paste back into your school email. Type the student's real name back in by hand. Then send.

✗ Never paste these into DeepL

Student legal names · Medical diagnoses · IEP or support plan details · Behavioural incident descriptions · Any information that could identify a specific student to an outside party.

✓ Safe to translate in DeepL

General parent letters with no student names · Classroom updates · Event notices · Homework instructions · Anything that does not name or identify a specific student.

Accessible Input Checklist — The Non-Tech Backup

🗣️ +
Reduce Idioms
Speak literally at all times
Idioms and figures of speech are invisible walls for ELL students. Replace every expression with its plain, literal meaning.
"That's a piece of cake."
"That is easy."
↓ Tap for real classroom scenarios
Scenario 1 — Math class You say: "Let's take a stab at this problem." A Level 1 student hears the word "stab" and looks confused or alarmed. Replace with: "Let's try this problem."
Scenario 2 — Giving encouragement You say: "You're on the right track!" A Level 2 student looks around for a literal track. Replace with: "Good work. That answer is correct."
Scenario 3 — Transition instructions You say: "Wrap it up and hit the road." Replace with: "Stop working. Pack your bag. Line up at the door."
Scenario 4 — Test expectations You say: "Give it your best shot." Replace with: "Try every question. Do as much as you can."
🧩 +
Increase Realia
Pair words with physical objects or gestures
Anchor new vocabulary to something the student can see, hold, or watch you do. Reduces cognitive load at all proficiency levels.
Say "pencil" → hold up a pencil.
Say "open your book" → mime the action.
↓ Tap for real classroom scenarios
Scenario 1 — Classroom supplies Introducing school supplies to a new student: hold up each item as you name it. Say "scissors" while holding scissors open and closed. Say "glue" while miming applying glue to paper. No translation needed.
Scenario 2 — Procedural instructions Instead of saying "fold the paper in half," physically fold your own paper while narrating each step slowly. The student can mirror the action without needing to process English first.
Scenario 3 — Calendar and time Point to the physical calendar, tap today's date, hold up fingers for the number of days until an event. Pair with the word: "Friday. Five days."
Scenario 4 — Feelings check-in Create a simple feelings chart with faces (happy, tired, sad, confused). Point to your own face and name the feeling. Student can point to the chart without speaking — giving you information and reducing their anxiety.
⏱️ +
Wait Time
5-second silent pause after every question
ELL students are processing in two languages simultaneously. A mandatory 5-second pause after asking a question gives the cognitive space needed to form a response.
Ask question → count silently to 5 → wait for response. Do not rephrase or redirect before 5 seconds.
↓ Tap for real classroom scenarios
Scenario 1 — Whole-class question You ask: "What do plants need to grow?" Immediately 10 hands go up. Wait 5 full seconds before taking any answer. This gives ELL students — who are still translating the question — the chance to formulate a response before the moment passes.
Scenario 2 — One-on-one check-in You ask a Level 2 student: "Do you understand?" They look at you blankly. The instinct is to rephrase immediately — resist it. Count silently to 5. The student is often processing and will nod or respond if given the moment.
Scenario 3 — Misread as not knowing A Level 3 student is called on and goes silent. Other students start calling out answers. This silence is often processing time, not ignorance. Gently hold the class and say: "We'll wait." The student frequently answers correctly given 5 seconds.
Scenario 4 — Using the Voice tool After using Google Translate Voice to explain a task, pause before asking if the student understood. The student has just heard a message in their language for the first time — give them a moment to process before expecting a verbal response.
How to use this with the translation tools
Levels 1–2
Use the Voice and Documents tools actively for most classroom communication. Translation support is essential at this stage.
Level 3
Shift toward scaffolded English with translation as a backup. Use tools for complex or subject-specific materials, not everyday communication.
Levels 4–5
Prioritise plain-language simplification over translation. Reserve the tools for family communication rather than in-class use.

1Open Google Translate. Set left to English, right to student's language.
2Click the 🎙️ on the left. Speak in English. Translation appears on screen.
3Press ⇄ Swap. Student clicks mic and speaks back. Read their words in English on the left.
⚠ Mic greyed out?IT system block — not a website error. Contact IT: System Settings → Privacy → Microphone.
📄 Use Case: Sending worksheets or forms home
02
Translate a document
translate.google.com/?op=docs
1Go to the site. Click the Documents tab.
2Click the upload box. Choose your PDF or Word file (under 10 MB).
3Set languages. Click Translate. Download as .docx (not PDF). Read before sharing.
⚠ Two things to watchFiles over 10 MB won't upload. Translation errors happen — always read the .docx before distributing.
